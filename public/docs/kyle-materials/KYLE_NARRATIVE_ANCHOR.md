## **THE SETUP: From Bartender to Staff Engineer**

"I want to start with context, because where you *come from* shapes how you approach tools like Claude.

I spent years in hospitality—bartending at O'Toole's, managing teams, quality control work. You learn something in that world: **systems and consistency matter more than heroics**. You can't scale a bar by having one brilliant bartender and five mediocre ones. You need a *recipe*—processes, feedback loops, continuous improvement.

That mindset followed me into tech.

I moved through quality control, then into automation and technical leadership. And about a year ago, I landed at Intinc as a Staff Engineer in AppSec and Platform Engineering, supporting 50 to 200 engineers. My job is to build paved roads—security frameworks, automation workflows, AI integration patterns—so engineers don't have to invent the wheel every time.

**And I have a daughter, Kinsley. I build educational applications for her—including a voice-to-illustrated book system that turns her imagination into stories with pictures.** That project taught me something crucial: AI doesn't replace judgment; it *scales* judgment if you architect it right.

That's the lens I'm bringing to Claude today."

## **THE INFLECTION: Finding the Right Tool**

"In late 2024, early 2025, I started experimenting with Claude.

Not because I was a hype-chaser. But because I was watching engineers and non-technical folks struggle with the same problem: **How do you work with AI without it becoming a black box or a time sink?**

I realized Claude was different. It wasn't just another chatbot. It was a *platform*—showing up everywhere. Web. Desktop. Mobile. CLI. API. Same intelligence, different friction points.

So I did what I always do: I decided to **fully map it**.

I learned the web interface—Projects, Artifacts, multi-turn reasoning. Then I went deeper: desktop apps with local MCP extensions connecting to my tools. Mobile voice for async thinking. The CLI for automation at scale. The API for building systems that run on my infrastructure, not Anthropic's.

And I built frameworks to make that learning *reproducible*:
- **R-I-S-E framework** (Role → Input → Stop condition → Deliver format)
- **F-L-O-W framework** (Focus → Layers → Outcomes → Waypoints)
- **Comprehensive prompt engineering patterns** for different contexts (research, coding, teaching, strategy)

I created the **AI Master Reference**—980 lines of living documentation showing patterns, guardrails, security-first methodologies. I built the **Platform Explorer v3.1**, showcasing how to architect AI integrations with security and compliance built in from day one. I created **Template Evaluation Academy** for consistent prompt quality at scale. And I developed **13 active MCP server configurations**, connecting Claude to GitHub, Stripe, n8n, Figma, Notion, and internal tools.

I also started building **FlashFusion**, an AI-powered SaaS platform for creators—brand kit generation, multi-channel publishing, workflow automation. All of this learned from what I was discovering about Claude.

**And here's what stunned me: the learning wasn't linear. It was exponential.**

Each new platform I mastered didn't replace the previous knowledge. It *compounded* it. Because I was learning the same underlying intelligence—Claude—in different contexts. So when I learned how to think clearly in one place (web), that thinking pattern worked *everywhere* (desktop, mobile, CLI, API).

The frameworks I built for web worked for CLI. The security patterns I learned for one MCP server scaled to 13. The prompting discipline I developed for research applied to code, communication, and teaching.

In about 4 months, I went from 'Claude is a tool' to 'Claude is a thinking partner I use for strategy, code, teaching, operations, and helping others do the same.'

**That compounding is what I'm here to show you.**"

## **THE PROOF: What Actually Changed**

"Let me be concrete. Here's the before-and-after:

### **Before (Traditional Approach)**

- **Research project?** 2–3 weeks. Gather sources manually. Synthesize findings. Validate claims (or hope they're right). Draft recommendations. Wait for feedback. Iterate.
- **Building something new?** Start from scratch or excavate old patterns from GitHub. Copy-paste code that *maybe* still works. Hope for the best.
- **Teaching a process?** Write a doc. Hope people read it. Answer questions via email 3 days later. Inconsistency spreads.
- **Bulk operations** (like analyzing 500 employee skill assessments)? Spreadsheet. Manual work. Error-prone. Weeks of effort.
- **Security & compliance?** Built on top, as an afterthought. "Let's review this for SOC2 later."

### **After (Claude, Used Deliberately)**

- **Research project?** 4–5 days. Claude synthesizes sources; I validate and judge. Every claim is sourced. Every trade-off is visible. I iterate *once*, not five times.
- **Building something?** Claude drafts; I review and validate. The final code is *better* because I've audited every decision. I understand why we made each choice (not just that we made it).
- **Teaching a process?** Claude learns the pattern *once* (via a Skill or CLAUDE.md file). *Everyone* benefits from that pattern. Consistency scales automatically.
- **Bulk operations?** One command. 2 minutes runtime. Structured output feeds into dashboards. What took a team a week takes an hour.
- **Security & compliance?** Built in from line one. Every prompt validates inputs. Every response filters outputs. Security isn't a review gate; it's the operating system.

**That's not just speed. That's a fundamental shift in *how I work*.**

But—and this is critical—it only works if you:

1. **Know what you're asking for.** Clarity of thought comes first. Tools amplify clarity; they don't create it.
2. **Validate the answer.** You remain the expert. Claude is the accelerant, not the authority.
3. **Iterate when needed.** The first answer isn't always the right one. Ask follow-ups. Refine. That's where the real insights emerge.
4. **Document the pattern.** So others can repeat it. Frameworks are how you scale judgment.

I did this consciously. It changed everything:
- How I work at Intinc (AppSec + Platform Engineering for 50–200 engineers)
- How I build for Kinsley (educational apps with voice, images, and interactivity)
- How I'm architecting FlashFusion (creator tools, commerce automation, multi-channel publishing)

**And that's what I want for your organization.**"

And I've learned the recovery patterns. How to validate sources. How to structure prompts so Claude shows its thinking. How to design systems where security is the default, not an afterthought.

And I've seen firsthand how the *right* approach to AI tools—transparent, iterative, security-first—compounds into culture change.

**For an OD manager specifically, this is everything.** Your job isn't to implement technology. Your job is to help humans *think better together*. And Claude—when used right—is one of the best tools for that I've ever seen.

Why? Because it forces clarity. It demands that you write down your assumptions. It shows you competing hypotheses. It makes you validate your claims. It teaches you to say 'I'm confident this is true (sources X, Y, Z)' versus 'I have a hunch.'

And when everyone in your organization is thinking like that—with rigor, sources, competing hypotheses, iterative refinement—your culture *changes*. Not because you have fancy software. But because people are thinking better.

That's the real shift."

## **TONE & DELIVERY NOTES**

### **How to Tell This Story**

- **Conversational, not polished.** You've lived this. Talk like you lived it. Pauses are fine. Genuine beats slick.
- **Show, don't tell.** As you mention artifacts, have them on screen:
  - Show a page from the AI Master Reference (980-line doc, structured)
  - Show the Platform Explorer v3.1 (proof of architecture)
  - Quick glimpse of Kinsley's voice-to-illustrated-book app (personal, relatable)
  - Show a prompt framework (R-I-S-E or F-L-O-W)

- **Vulnerability builds credibility.** Mention a failure:
  - "I built a system that passed secrets into prompts by accident—twice. Then I fixed it. Now I validate everything."
  - "Claude hallucinated a source that *sounded* right but didn't exist. That's when I learned to require multi-source verification."
  - "I over-automated and introduced new risks. Had to step back, re-architect."
  
  Failures show you've *actually* done this, not just watched YouTube.

- **Pacing:** Build *slowly*. Don't rush to the wow. The wow is in the journey, not the destination. Take 3–4 minutes here. Don't skip the bartending story; that's where your systems thinking comes from.

- **Eye contact.** Talk to the audience, not the slides. This is a conversation where you happen to have visuals, not a presentation where you happen to talk.
